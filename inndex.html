1. Concepts of Phonetics and Phonology

Phonetics is the study of the physical sounds of speech – how they are produced and heard.

Phonology studies how sounds function in language – the rules and patterns of sound use.

2. Places of Articulation

These refer to the point in the mouth where the sound is produced. Examples include:

  • Bilabial: /p/, /b/
  • Alveolar: /t/, /d/
  • Velar: /k/, /g/

3. Articulators Involved in Sound Production

The organs involved in speech include:

  • Lips
  • Teeth
  • Tongue
  • Alveolar ridge
  • Hard & soft palate
  • Glottis

4. Voiced and Unvoiced Sounds

Voiced: Vocal cords vibrate (e.g., /b/, /d/, /g/)

Unvoiced: No vibration (e.g., /p/, /t/, /k/)

5. Content Words

Words that carry meaning (nouns, main verbs, adjectives, adverbs).

Examples: apple, run

6. Function Words

Words that serve a grammatical purpose (articles, prepositions, conjunctions).

Examples: the, in

7. Intonation

Falling Intonation

Used in statements and WH-questions. The pitch drops at the end.

  • She is from Canada.
  • What time is it?

Rising Intonation

Used in yes/no questions or to show uncertainty. The pitch rises at the end.

  • Do you like it?
  • Are we late?
