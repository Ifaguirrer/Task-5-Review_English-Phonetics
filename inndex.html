English Phonetics & Phonology: A Complete Guide

Introduction

Phonetics and phonology are two closely related fields of linguistics that study speech sounds. While phonetics focuses on the physical properties of sounds — how they are produced and heard — phonology studies how sounds function within a language’s system.

1. Places of Articulation

Places of articulation refer to where in the mouth or vocal tract a sound is produced by obstructing airflow.

  • Bilabial: Both lips come together to make sounds like /p/, /b/, and /m/. Example: pat, bat, mat.
  • Dental: Tongue touches upper teeth, as in /θ/ (“thin”) and /ð/ (“this”).
  • Alveolar: Tongue touches the ridge behind the upper teeth, producing /t/, /d/, /s/, /z/, /n/, /l/.
  • Palatal: Tongue contacts the hard palate, such as in /ʃ/ (“she”), /ʒ/ (“measure”).
  • Velar: Back of the tongue touches the soft palate, making /k/, /g/, /ŋ/ (“sing”).

2. Articulators Involved in Sound Production

Different organs in the vocal tract work together to produce speech sounds:

  • Lips: Shape and open/close to produce sounds.
  • Teeth: Used in dental sounds.
  • Alveolar Ridge: Important for alveolar sounds.
  • Hard and Soft Palate: Help shape airflow.
  • Tongue: Most versatile articulator, changes shape and position.
  • Glottis: Space between vocal cords, used in glottal sounds.

3. Voiced vs. Unvoiced Sounds

Voiced sounds use the vibration of vocal cords; unvoiced sounds do not.

Try placing your hand on your throat while saying these pairs:

  • Voiced: /b/, /d/, /g/, /z/, /v/, /ʒ/
  • Unvoiced: /p/, /t/, /k/, /s/, /f/, /ʃ/
Example: Feel the vibration in z versus the lack of vibration in s.

4. Content Words vs. Function Words

Content Words

Carry the main meaning in sentences:

  • Nouns: dog, city
  • Verbs: run, think
  • Adjectives: blue, happy
  • Adverbs: quickly, well

Examples: teacher, play

Function Words

Help connect and structure sentences:

  • Prepositions: in, on, at
  • Articles: the, a
  • Pronouns: he, they
  • Conjunctions: and, but

Examples: and, the

5. Intonation Patterns

Falling Intonation

Common in statements, commands, and WH-questions. The pitch falls at the end.

Examples:
1. She is coming home.
2. Where are you going?

Rising Intonation

Used in yes/no questions and when showing surprise. The pitch rises at the end.

Examples:
1. Are you ready?
2. Really?

6. Practice Exercise

Try these exercises to practice:

  1. Say the words pat and bat. Which one is voiced?
  2. Identify the content and function words in the sentence: The quick brown fox jumps over the lazy dog.
  3. Practice rising intonation with the question: Do you like music?
  4. Listen to yourself saying sss and zzz. Feel the difference in vocal cord vibration.
