üî∏ Concepts of Phonetics and Phonology

Phonetics is the study of the physical sounds of human speech. It looks at how sounds are produced and heard. Phonology focuses on how sounds function within a particular language or languages.

üî∏ Places of Articulation

This refers to where in the vocal tract the airflow is restricted to produce sounds. Examples include:

  • Bilabial: both lips (e.g., /p/, /b/)
  • Alveolar: tongue touches the ridge behind teeth (e.g., /t/, /d/)
  • Velar: back of the tongue against the soft palate (e.g., /k/, /g/)

üî∏ Articulators Involved in Sound Production

Key speech organs involved in articulation:

  • Lips
  • Teeth
  • Alveolar ridge
  • Palate
  • Velum (soft palate)
  • Glottis
  • Tongue

üî∏ Voiced and Unvoiced Sounds

Voiced sounds are produced when the vocal cords vibrate (e.g., /z/, /b/, /g/). Unvoiced sounds are made without vocal cord vibration (e.g., /s/, /p/, /k/).

üî∏ Content Words

Content words carry the main meaning in a sentence. They include:

  • Nouns
  • Verbs
  • Adjectives
  • Adverbs
Examples: Teacher, run

üî∏ Function Words

Function words help us connect content words and give structure to sentences. They include:

  • Prepositions
  • Articles
  • Pronouns
  • Conjunctions
Examples: and, the

üî∏ Falling Intonation

Falling intonation is used in statements and WH-questions. The pitch of the voice falls at the end of the sentence.

Examples:
1. What time is it?
2. She is a teacher.

üî∏ Rising Intonation

Rising intonation is common in yes/no questions. The pitch of the voice rises at the end of the sentence.

Examples:
1. Are you okay?
2. Do you like pizza?
